## Chapter: Job Stress (Unit 10)

### 10.1 What is Stress?

- **Selye's definition:** Stress = non-specific response of the body to any demand made on it
- **Origin of the word:** Borrowed from engineering — force/pressure on a material that resists it
- **Stress as stimulus:** External force beyond individual's control (Weitz's stressful stimuli: threat, isolation, frustration, group pressure, etc.)
- **Stress as response:** Body's physiological reaction demanding physical/psychological adaptation

#### General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) — 3 Stages

```
Duration of Stress →

[ALARM STAGE]         [RESISTANCE STAGE]      [EXHAUSTION STAGE]
- Shock phase          - Max adaptation         - Adaptation energy gone
- Countershock phase   - Resistance above       - Resistance declines
- Adrenalin surge        normal                 - Organism collapses
```

- **Alarm:** Initial shock → defensive mechanisms activate (high heart rate, adrenalin, ulcers)
- **Resistance:** Body fights back; bodily alarm signs disappear; peak adaptation
- **Exhaustion:** Energy depleted; alarm signs return; irreversible decline
- **Shortcoming of GAS:** Research was on animals, not always applicable to humans

#### Approaches to Understanding Stress

- **External Force approach:** Stress is an independent variable caused by outside conditions
- **Interactive approach (McGrath):** Stress = gap between environmental demand and person's capacity to meet it
- **Comprehensive approach:** Stress is both interactive AND unique to the individual + their environment

### 10.2 Understanding Stress in Organisations

- **Word origin:** Latin *strictus* = tight/narrow
- **Stressor:** Any person or event triggering the stress response (e.g., job interview, doctor visit)
- **Stress reactivity:** Short-term physical reactions (high BP, fast heart rate, sweating)
- **Strain/Distress:** When stress reactions persist beyond the body's tolerance → negative consequences

#### Types of Stress by Quality

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Eustress** | Positive stress; energises, improves performance | Exam anxiety motivating study |
| **Distress** | Negative stress; demands exceed coping ability | Last-chance job interview |
| **Hypostress** | Too little stress; boredom, no challenge | Monotonous, unchallenging job |
| **Hyperstress** | Too much stress; events pile up beyond adaptability | Role overload |

#### Inverted-U Theory (Stress vs. Performance)

```
Performance
    HIGH |        /\
         |       /  \
         |      /    \
    LOW  |_____/      \______
         LOW   MID    HIGH
              Stress/Pressure

→ Moderate stress = BEST performance
→ Too low (boredom) or too high (anxiety) = LOW performance
```

- **Key finding:** High distress → lower job satisfaction + higher job turnover (study of 1800 US managers)
- **Eustress** had NO relationship to job satisfaction in the same study

### 10.3 Types of Stress

Three categories: **(1) Psychological, (2) Organisational, (3) Societal**

#### Psychological Stressors (Lazarus & Cohen — 3 categories)

- **Cataclysmic phenomena:** Sudden events affecting many people (natural disasters, wars)
- **Individual crises:** Events affecting fewer people but equally disruptive (death of a loved one)
- **Daily hassles:** Stable, repetitive problems in daily life (routine work frustrations)

#### Organisational Stressors — Pareek's 10 Role Stresses

| # | Role Stress | Abbreviation | What it means |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Inter-role Distance | IRD | Conflict between work role and personal role (e.g., executive vs. husband) |
| 2 | Role Stagnation | RS | Feeling stuck in the same role; no career growth |
| 3 | Role Expectation Conflict | REC | Different people expect different things from the same role |
| 4 | Role Erosion | RE | Your functions are taken away or credit goes to someone else |
| 5 | Role Overload | RO | Too many expectations; quantitative (too much) or qualitative (too hard) |
| 6 | Role Isolation | RI | Psychological distance between your role and other roles in the team |
| 7 | Personal Inadequacy | PI | Feeling you lack the skills to perform your role |
| 8 | Self-Role Distance | SRD | Your role conflicts with your self-concept/identity |
| 9 | Role Ambiguity | RA | Unclear expectations about your role responsibilities |
| 10 | Resource Inadequacy | RIn | Not given enough resources to perform your role |

#### Societal Stressors

- Culture shapes what people define as stressful (e.g., infant death is catastrophic in one culture, spiritually neutral in another)
- Family issues (marital conflict, illness, relocation) spill over into work stress
- Wider environment (taxes, shortages, public systems) creates impersonal but real stress

### 10.4 Stress Management Techniques

#### I. Personal Strategies

- **Work-life balance:** Overworking long-term is unhealthy; balance prevents stress build-up
- **Positive mindset & resilience:** Cognitive appraisal shapes whether an event feels stressful; Seligman's **PERMA** model (Positive emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment)
- **Social support:** Acts as a buffer; laughter at work reduces stress and boosts productivity
- **Time management:** Setting priorities prevents deadline panic; poor time management = constant rush
- **Coping skills:** Focus on what you CAN control, not what you cannot
- **Leisure activities:** Promote joy and reduce tension; any hobby helps
- **Physical exercise:** Aerobic exercise reduces stress reactivity; even walking helps
- **Diet:** High sugar/cholesterol foods alter blood chemistry and increase stress vulnerability
- **Professional help:** Seek early — warning signs include appetite loss, sleep issues, mood swings

#### II. Organisational Strategies

- **Job redesign:** Increase worker control (job decision latitude) → reduces uncertainty and stress
- **Goal setting:** Channels energy productively; focuses attention and motivates

### 10.5 Burnout and Rustout

#### BOSS — Burnout Stress Syndrome

- **Definition:** Debilitating psychological condition from unrelieved work stress
- **Result of:** High job stress + personal frustration + poor coping skills

#### 4 Stages of Burnout

```
[HONEYMOON]  →  [FUEL SHORTAGE]  →  [CHRONIC SYMPTOMS]  →  [CRISIS]  →  [HIT THE WALL]
Excitement,      Fatigue, job         Exhaustion,            Peptic ulcers,   Total collapse,
energy depletion  dissatisfaction,    anger, illness,        depression,       career ends,
forms bad habits  sleep issues        depression             escape mentality  loss of control
```

- **Honeymoon:** New job excitement; energy drains silently; bad coping habits form
- **Fuel shortage:** Vague loss, fatigue, inefficiency; escape via eating/drinking/smoking
- **Chronic symptoms:** Physical illness, anger, depression become pronounced
- **Crisis:** Pessimism, ulcers, headaches, high blood pressure, insomnia
- **Hitting the wall:** All energy depleted; professional collapse; loss of life control

#### Burnout Definition (Pareek)

- Burnout = stress experienced but NOT properly coped with → exhaustion, irritability, health problems (hypertension, ulcers, heart disease)
- **Glow-up** (opposite) = stress properly channeled → satisfaction, creativity, effectiveness

#### Personality Factors in Burnout

- **Type A personality** → associated with burnout (especially cynicism + loneliness)
- **Externality, low self-esteem, rigidity, alienation, Machiavellianism** → contribute to burnout
- **High impulse control/self-control** → higher stress tolerance

#### ROSS — Rustout Stress Syndrome

- **Opposite of BOSS:** Stress *underload* — person is capable of more than what is required
- Related to **role erosion**
- Can be qualitative (work is too easy) or quantitative (too little work)

### 10.6 Coping with Stress

#### Two Approaches to Coping

```
APPROACH (Functional)                AVOIDANCE (Dysfunctional)
---------------------------          ---------------------------
Face the problem head-on             Deny or escape the stress
Negotiate, collaborate               Use alcohol, drugs, ignore
Leads to growth                      Short-term relief, long-term damage
```

- **Coping traits:** Stable personal tendencies in how someone handles stress (assessed via personality tests)
- **Coping styles:** Broader disposition; similar to traits
- **Active coping:** Face the stress and negotiate/clarify
- **Passive coping:** Suffer or deny the stress

#### Coping Strategies for 10 Role Stresses (Pareek)

| Role Stress | Dysfunctional Strategy | Functional Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Self-role distance | Role rejection / self rejection | **Role integration** |
| Inter-role distance | Role partition / role elimination | **Role negotiation** |
| Role stagnation | Role fixation | **Role transition** |
| Role isolation | Role boundness | **Role linkage** |
| Role ambiguity | Role prescription | **Role clarification** |
| Role expectation conflict | Role taking | **Role making** |
| Role overload | Role reduction | **Role slimming** |
| Role erosion | Role visibility | **Role development/enrichment** |
| Resource inadequacy | Role atrophy | **Resource generation** |
| Personal inadequacy | Role shrinkage | **Role linkage** |

#### Key Coping Concepts

- **Role integration:** Align self-concept with role; grow into the role OR bring yourself into it
- **Role negotiation:** Mutually agree on how to share/balance conflicting role demands
- **Role transition:** Consciously let go of old role and embrace new one (like growing from child to adult)
- **Role slimming:** Delegate overloaded functions to others so they grow too
- **Role making:** Creatively redefine an ambiguous role using your own strengths

#### What Organisations Can Do (Stress Audit)

- **Stress audit:** Study, explore, and control stresses experienced by executives in the org
- Three audit variables: **(1) Causal stressors → (2) Mediating factors (personality/culture) → (3) End results (stress reactions)**
- Use questionnaires, interviews, and physical exams to collect data
- **Use scientific inputs:** Educate employees on diet, exercise, meditation
- **Company doctor:** Deploy medical officers to stress management programs
- **Spread the message:** Promote healthy habits — regular work, leisure, diet, exercise, mental peace

#### Individual Safety Valves (Gmelch's 5 Strategies + Safety Valves)

**Gmelch (1982) — 5 Strategies:**
- Take a hard look at yourself — identify your stress sources
- Stay alert — find meaning outside of work
- Take risks — moderate risk-taking builds productivity
- Avoid isolation — maintain relationships with colleagues and family
- Stretch for success — keep working toward goals despite obstacles
- Overcome obsolescence — keep skills updated for changing environments

**Safety Valves:**
- **Changing gears:** Shift attention to a hobby or non-work activity (intrinsic motivators)
- **Cut back on overtime:** More hours = higher burnout risk
- **Exercise:** Acts as stress inoculation; even walking works; relieves pressure AND prepares you for next day
- **Pamper yourself:** Break routine; take mini-vacations; plan enjoyable activities
- **Get involved:** Boring jobs cause burnout too; seek stimulating engagement
- **Warm up slowly:** Use the 2 hours before work to set a calm, prepared tone
- **Release job pressure:** Tackle hard tasks when fresh, not when tired
- **Practice relaxation:** Switch off the stress response consciously (yoga, meditation, deep breathing)
