## Unit 3: Evolution of Organizational Behaviour

**Overview:** Traces how OB developed from early industrial thinkers through classical management, human relations, and behavioural science. Covers key theorists, theories, and their relevance to modern management.

---

### 3.1 Early Influencers (Pre-Classical)

- **Adam Smith** → introduced *Division of Labour* (work specialization) to boost productivity and skill
- **Charles Babbage** → extended Smith's ideas; specialization reduces learning time, cuts waste, raises skill levels, and matches people to tasks
- **Robert Owen** → first industrialist to fight for workers' rights; pushed for regulated hours, child labour laws, public education, and workplace meals (100+ years ahead of his time)

---

### 3.2 The Classical Movement (1900–mid 1930s)

**Overview:** First formal management theories. Focus on structure, logic, rules, and efficiency. Key names: Taylor, Fayol, Weber.

---

#### Scientific Management – F.W. Taylor ("Father of Scientific Management")

- Find the **one best way** to do each job using scientific analysis
- Break jobs into tasks → time each → build the most efficient sequence
- Key principles: scientific job design, scientific selection & training, worker-management cooperation, division of work

**Evaluation:**
- ❌ Repetitive work, workers treated like machines, unions hated it, managers got too much power
- ✅ Better wages, less burnout, skills matched to jobs, improved coordination

---

#### Administrative Theory – Henri Fayol

- All managers perform **5 functions**: Plan → Organise → Command → Coordinate → Control
- Proposed **14 Principles of Management:**

| # | Principle | Key Idea |
|---|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Division of Labour | Split work for efficiency |
| 2 | Authority | Right to order = responsibility |
| 3 | Discipline | Respect rules & agreements |
| 4 | Unity of Command | One boss per employee |
| 5 | Unity of Direction | One plan per goal |
| 6 | Subordination | Org goals > personal goals |
| 7 | Remuneration | Fair pay for all |
| 8 | Centralisation | Balance power between top & bottom |
| 9 | Scalar Chain | Clear hierarchy line |
| 10 | Order | Right person, right place, right time |
| 11 | Equity | Treat employees with kindness & fairness |
| 12 | Stability of Tenure | Low turnover = better performance |
| 13 | Initiative | Encourage creativity in employees |
| 14 | Esprit de Corps | Teamwork + harmony + open communication |

---

#### Structural Theory (Bureaucracy) – Max Weber

- Identified the **bureaucratic organisation**: expertise + discipline + formal rules
- Key features: official job duties, clear hierarchy, formal rules, employment by technical qualification

**Stewart's 4 features of Bureaucracy:**
- *Specialization* – job continuity even if worker leaves
- *Hierarchy of authority* – clear management vs worker levels
- *System of rules* – formal, efficient work processes
- *Impersonality* – authority by rules, not personal preference

**Criticism of Bureaucracy:**
- Too rigid, too many rules, secretive, insensitive to employees, executives abuse status

**Modern Alternatives (Ridderstrale):**
- Flatter structures, flexible cross-functional movement, learning organisations, knowledge sharing

```
Classical Approach Flow:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Taylor (How to work best) → Scientific Management
  Fayol  (How to manage)   → Administrative Theory
  Weber  (How to structure) → Bureaucracy
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**Classical Approach – Evaluation:**
- ✅ First systematic view of management; universal principles; focus on effectiveness
- ❌ Ignores personality, social factors, situational differences; too rigid

**Structuralism** → bridge between classical and human relations; considers both formal AND informal aspects of organisations.

---

#### Mooney & Reiley – 3 Principles

- **Coordination principle** – unity of action; everyone works together under authority
- **Scalar principle** – hierarchy, job-level responsibilities, delegation
- **Functional principle** – every task specialised; roles clearly differentiated

---

### 3.3 Social Man Theory

- Taylor/Fayol/Weber ignored the human/social side → **Mary Parker Follett** and **Chester Barnard** fixed this (ideas recognised from 1930s onwards)

#### Mary Parker Follett

- Management = *"the art of getting things done through others"*
- **Conflict** → best resolved by *integration* (not domination or compromise)
- **Power** → should be shared (power-with, not power-over); flows from skill, not rank
- **Orders** → should come from work demands, not just hierarchy; everyone must understand the organisation's purpose
- **Authority** → comes from knowledge + responsibility, not just position; decisions should be joint
- **Leadership** → function-based, not position-based; leaders guide groups toward common goals
- **Management as profession** → apply scientific approach; train managers professionally

#### Chester Barnard

- Organisations = **social systems** needing human cooperation
- Manager's main role: communicate + influence employees to meet goals
- **Formal org** = deliberately created to achieve goals; **Informal org** = grows naturally from personal bonds
- **Decision Making** → narrow choices to manageable options; rational process; depends on urgency and strategic factors
- **Executive functions** → instil moral purpose, foster cooperation, put team above self-interest
- **Responsibility & Morality** → moral codes guide individual behaviour in conflict situations

```
Barnard's Org Model:
┌────────────────────────────────┐
│       FORMAL ORGANISATION      │
│  (deliberate, goal-directed)   │
│  ┌──────────────────────────┐  │
│  │  INFORMAL ORGANISATION   │  │
│  │ (natural, social bonds)  │  │
│  └──────────────────────────┘  │
└────────────────────────────────┘
Both must work together for cooperation & effectiveness
```

### 3.4 Behavioural Movement

- Human side of workers started getting attention in this era
- Two key precursors: **Personnel Office** and **Industrial Psychology**

#### Personnel Office
- Firms created "welfare secretary" roles → early HR departments
- Goodrich Co. (1900): first employment dept (hiring only)
- NCR (1902): first full labour dept – wages, grievances, health, records

#### Industrial Psychology – Hugo Munsterberg (1913)
- Scientific study of human behaviour to find patterns and individual differences
- Linked scientific management with psychology
- Advocated: psychological tests for hiring, training for development, studying motivation

#### The Wagner Act (1935) – "Magna Carta of Labour"
- Followed 1929 stock market crash and Great Depression
- Gave unions legal right to bargain collectively with employers
- Improved working conditions → better union-management relations → led to the Human Relations Movement

---

### 3.5 Human Relations Movement

- Core belief: **higher employee satisfaction = higher productivity**
- Key figures: Dale Carnegie, Abraham Maslow, Douglas McGregor

#### Hawthorne Experiments (1924–1932) – Elton Mayo, Western Electric Co., Chicago

**4 Phases:**

```
Phase 1: Illumination Experiments
  → Changed light levels → no change in productivity
  → Conclusion: physical conditions ≠ productivity

Phase 2: Relay Assembly Test Room
  → 6 women, 13 test periods, changed work conditions
  → Productivity kept rising → because of attention from management
  → Famous result: "HAWTHORNE EFFECT"

Phase 3: Interviewing Programme
  → 20,000+ interviews → understood workers' feelings, needs, attitudes
  → Showed importance of listening to workers

Phase 4: Bank Wiring Observation Room
  → 14 men observed → informal groups formed, natural leaders emerged
  → Group set its own output level despite financial incentives
  → Peer pressure > money
```

**Hawthorne Effect** = workers perform better when they feel they are being watched/cared for by management

**Importance:** New ideas on teams, leadership, group communication, job design, motivation

**Criticism:** No focus on environment, lacks scientific rigour, gender differences between phases not accounted for

#### Dale Carnegie
- Success = winning cooperation of others
- 4 tips: make people feel important, make good first impressions, let others talk, praise good traits

---

### 3.6 Neo-Human Relations (1950s–1960s)

- Focus shifted to individual motivation and leadership styles after Hawthorne
- Emphasised personal adjustment, group dynamics, psychological needs

#### Abraham Maslow (1943) – Hierarchy of Needs

```
        /\
       /  \  Self-Actualisation (highest)
      /────\
     / Esteem\
    /──────────\
   /  Love/Social\
  /────────────────\
 /   Safety Needs   \
/────────────────────\
   Physiological      (lowest)
```

- People move up only after lower need is satisfied
- Directly influenced motivation theory and job/work design

#### Douglas McGregor – Theory X vs Theory Y

| | Theory X | Theory Y |
|---|---|---|
| View of workers | Lazy, dislike work | Self-motivated, responsible |
| Management style | Control, punish, micromanage | Participative, trust-based |
| Result | Low morale | Collaboration & growth |

**Neo-Human Relations led to:** focus on group dynamics, job satisfaction, interpersonal relations, conflict management, employee relations

### 3.7 Behavioral Science Theorists

- Used **objective scientific research** to study human behaviour in organisations

| Theorist | Contribution | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Jacob Moreno** | Sociometric analysis – maps attraction/repulsion in groups | Build cohesive teams |
| **B.F. Skinner** | Operant conditioning – behaviour shaped by consequences | Design of training & reward systems |
| **David McClelland** | Projective tests – identify high vs low need to achieve | Match people to jobs, enrich roles |
| **Fred Fiedler** | Contingency model of leadership (mid 1960s–late 1970s) | Leadership depends on situation |
| **Herzberg** | Two-factor theory: motivators vs hygiene factors | Job design & motivation strategy |
| **Hackman & Oldham** | 5 core job dimensions → high performance & satisfaction | Job design tool |

#### Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

```
Hygiene Factors (when ABSENT → dissatisfaction):
  salary, job security, work conditions, company policy

Motivators (when PRESENT → motivation & satisfaction):
  achievement, recognition, growth, responsibility, job content
```

#### Hackman & Oldham – 5 Core Job Dimensions
1. Skill variety
2. Task identity
3. Task significance
4. Autonomy
5. Feedback

### 3.8 Summary – Evolution at a Glance

```
Early Influencers         Classical Era           Human Side Era
(Smith, Babbage, Owen) → (Taylor, Fayol, Weber) → (Follett, Barnard,
Division of Labour        Structure & Rules         Mayo, Maslow,
Work Specialization       Scientific Mgmt           McGregor, Skinner)
                          Bureaucracy               Human Relations
                                                    Behavioural Science
```

**Key takeaway:** No single approach is complete. Each builds on the previous. Managers should draw from multiple approaches depending on the problem at hand.

---

### Relevance of OB Theories – Quick Points

**Advantages:**
- Gives a framework to view management and compare practices
- Helps identify problem areas in organisations
- Enables managers to pick the right approach for each situation

**Cautions:**
- Approaches are complementary, not competing
- Earlier ideas still matter today
- "Schools" is too rigid a term — "approaches" is more accurate
