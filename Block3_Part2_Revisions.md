## Defining Conflict

- Conflict = natural disagreement between people/groups due to different attitudes, beliefs, values, or needs.
- Has **both positive and negative** sides — it can help OR hurt performance.
- Common causes: poor communication, scarce resources, interpersonal issues, leadership problems.
- Formal definition: *"A process that begins when one party perceives that another has negatively affected something it cares about."*

### Three Views on Conflict

```
TRADITIONAL (1930s–40s)  →  BEHAVIOURAL (1940s–70s)  →  INTERACTIONIST (Current)
"All conflict is bad"         "Conflict is inevitable"      "Conflict can be good"
Avoid & eliminate it          Accept but resolve it quickly  Manage & sometimes stimulate it
```

- **Traditional View:** Conflict = management failure; must be eliminated.
- **Behavioural View:** Conflict is unavoidable; manager's job is to restore trust.
- **Interactionist View (current):** Some conflict keeps groups creative; too little = stagnation.

## Types and Levels of Conflict

### By Level (Who is involved)

- **Intra-individual:** Conflict within one person (e.g., approach-avoidance, approach-approach, avoidance-avoidance).
- **Interpersonal:** Between two people, due to personality clashes or task disagreements.
- **Individual vs. Group:** Person deviates from group norms and faces pressure.
- **Inter-group:** Between departments or unions (e.g., Production vs. Marketing).
- **Inter-organisational:** Between companies; considered healthy in a free market.

### By Type (Classification Schemes)

| Scheme | Types |
|--------|-------|
| I | Functional (constructive) vs. Dysfunctional (destructive) |
| II | Task / Relationship / Process |
| III | Intrinsic (rational) vs. Extrinsic (emotional) |
| IV | Realistic (goal-based) vs. Non-realistic (emotion-based) |
| V | Substantive (task) vs. Affective (interpersonal) |

- **Inter-role conflict:** Contradictory expectations in different roles.
- **Person-role conflict:** Role demands clash with personal values.

## Sources of Conflict

- **Competition for limited resources** — groups fight over money, power, staff time.
- **Diversity of goals** — different departments have conflicting priorities.
- **Task interdependence** — groups depend on each other; delays cause friction.
  - *Pooled:* Groups don't interact directly but share outcomes.
  - *Sequential:* Group B can't start until Group A finishes.
  - *Reciprocal:* Groups mutually depend on each other (highest conflict risk).
- **Differences in values & perception** — e.g., labour vs. management on profit sharing.
- **Organisational ambiguity** — unclear job roles and responsibilities.
- **Introduction of change** — mergers, acquisitions create power struggles.
- **Communication problems** — too little OR too much information causes conflict.
- **Aggressive personalities** — authoritarian, dogmatic, or arrogant individuals.

## Effects of Conflict

### Within Groups (during conflict)
- Group cohesion increases.
- Group becomes more task-focused.
- Leadership becomes more directive/authoritarian.
- Organisational structure becomes rigid.

### Between Groups (prolonged conflict)
- Groups become enemies.
- Perceptions get distorted (own group = good; other = bad).
- Communication stops.
- Each group applies double standards.

### Conflict vs. Performance (inverted U curve)

```
HIGH  |        *
      |      *   *
PERF  |    *       *
      |  *           *
LOW   |*               *
      +------------------
      LOW    OPT    HIGH
           CONFLICT
      
  Too low = stagnation
  Optimal = creativity + self-criticism
  Too high = chaos + disruption
```

### Quick Reference: Consequences

| Positive | Negative |
|----------|----------|
| New ideas, creativity | Diverts energy from work |
| Motivates change | Threatens psychological health |
| Clarifies real issues | Wastes resources |
| Acts as emotional release | Breaks group cohesion |

## Conflict Process

### 4-Stage Model (Robbins)

```
Stage I          Stage II           Stage III              Stage IV
Potential   -->  Cognition &   -->  Conflictive       -->  Aftermath
Antagonism       Personalisation    Behaviour              (Consequences
(Sources)        (Perceived/Felt    (Manifest actions)      feed back into
                  conflict)          + Management)          Stage I)
```

- **Stage I:** Antecedent conditions exist (resource scarcity, goal differences, etc.).
- **Stage II:** Conditions are *perceived* as threatening → felt emotionally (hostility, fear).
- **Stage III:** Behaviour erupts (strikes, aggression, or managed responses).
- **Stage IV:** Outcome affects future conflict probability.

### Forsyth's 5-Stage Model

```
Routine Interaction
       ↓
  Disagreement
       ↓
  Confrontation
       ↓
   Escalation
       ↓
  De-escalation
       ↓
   Resolution
```

## Conflict Management Frameworks

### Thomas's Framework (2 Dimensions × 5 Styles)

```
          HIGH
           |  COMPETING          COLLABORATING
ASSERTIVE  |  (Win at all costs)  (Win-win)
           |         COMPROMISING
UNASSERTIVE|  AVOIDING           ACCOMMODATING
           |  (Withdraw)         (Self-sacrifice)
           +----------------------------
           UNCOOPERATIVE      COOPERATIVE
```

- **Competing:** Satisfy own interests, ignore others.
- **Collaborating:** Best outcome; both parties fully satisfied.
- **Avoiding:** Withdraw or suppress the conflict.
- **Accommodating:** Give in to keep the relationship.
- **Compromising:** Each party gives something up.

### Pareek's Framework (8 Styles)

**Avoidance Modes (based on fear):**
- **Resignation** — feel helpless, do nothing.
- **Withdrawal** — physically remove yourself.
- **Defusion** — buy time, let things cool.
- **Appeasement** — offer small concessions to buy temporary peace.

**Approach Modes (solution-focused):**
- **Confrontation** — fight for your preferred solution (win-lose trap).
- **Compromise** — share the gain without fully solving the problem.
- **Arbitration** — bring in a neutral third party to decide.
- **Negotiation** — both sides jointly explore solutions (best outcome).

### Contingency Framework

- **Collaboration** → best when parties are interdependent and trust is high.
- **Bargaining** → best for allocating scarce resources or reaching formal agreements.
- **Power play** → best when one party has clear power advantage.
- **Negotiation** → used when both parties are ready and willing to resolve constructively.

## Conflict Prevention

### When to Stimulate Conflict (signs of too little conflict)
- "Yes-men" culture, fear of admitting mistakes, over-emphasis on harmony.
- Low creativity, resistance to change, unusually low staff turnover.

### How to Stimulate Conflict
1. **Manipulate communication** — send ambiguous messages, deviate from normal channels.
2. **Change structure** — increase unit size, add interdependence between groups.
3. **Change behaviour** — introduce role conflict, change leadership style.

### Conflict Resolution Strategies (Feldman)

| Strategy | Openness | Best Used When |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| Avoidance (Ignoring/Imposing) | Low | Trivial issue or urgent decision needed |
| Defusion (Smoothing/Superordinate goals) | Medium | Emotions need cooling |
| Containment (Representatives/Bargaining) | Medium | Structured discussion needed |
| Confrontation (Problem-solving/Redesign) | High | Important issue, time available |

## Negotiation

- **Negotiation** = process of reaching a mutual agreement between two or more parties.
- Key skills: active listening, persuasion, preparation, flexibility.
- Needed more today due to: diverse workforce, global business, team-based structures.

---

## Process of Negotiation

### 4 Common Elements (Lax & Sebenius, 1986)
1. Parties are **interdependent**.
2. Parties are in **conflict** over goals/processes.
3. Parties can **influence** each other.
4. Parties **believe** an agreement is possible.

### 4 Phases

```
Phase 1              Phase 2           Phase 3         Phase 4
Investigation  --->  Presentation --->  Bargaining --->  Agreement
& Preparation        (Initial offers)   (Exchange)       (Final terms)
      ^                                                       |
      |_______________________________________________________|
                   (Process is continuous/cyclical)
```

- **Phase 1:** Gather facts + assess BATNA.
- **Phase 2:** Present opening position clearly (verbal + non-verbal).
- **Phase 3:** Use communication skills to reach mutual ground.
- **Phase 4:** Finalise terms acceptable to both parties.

### BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)

> *Concept by Roger Fisher & William Ury — "Getting to Yes"*

- Your BATNA = what you'll do **if** the negotiation fails.
- **Better BATNA = stronger negotiating position.**
- Never reveal your BATNA to the other party.

**Mini Example:**
> You're negotiating a salary of ₹8L. Your BATNA = another offer at ₹7L.
> If the employer offers ₹7.5L → reject (worse than BATNA).
> If the employer offers ₹8.5L → accept (better than BATNA).

**BATNA Best Practices:**
1. List all alternatives if negotiation fails.
2. Work on improving those alternatives.
3. Keep your best alternative in reserve.
4. Update your BATNA as situations change.
5. Never disclose your BATNA.
6. Thorough preparation = better outcomes.

## Types of Bargaining Strategies

### Distributive vs. Integrative Bargaining

| Feature | Distributive | Integrative |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Goal | Max own share | Distribute evenly |
| Motivation | Win-lose | Win-win |
| Focus | Position | Interest |
| Information Sharing | Low | High |
| Relationship Duration | Short-term | Long-term |

### Distributive Bargaining (Zero-Sum)

```
Party A's range  |  Settlement  |  Party B's range
<----------------|     Zone     |---------------->
A's target    A's resistance  B's resistance  B's target
```

- Each party has a **resistance point** (lowest acceptable outcome).
- If ranges overlap → **settlement zone** exists.
- Best tactic: **make the first offer** and make it aggressive.
- Example: Union vs. Management wage negotiation.

### Integrative Bargaining (Win-Win)

```
Positional                    Integrative
-----------                   -----------
Adversaries          vs.      Joint problem-solvers
Fight over position            Focus on interests
Demand concessions             Work together
Mislead & pressure             Use fair principles
Win for you alone              Look for win-win
```

- **Why integrative is better:** More satisfying, builds long-term relationships, reduces chaos.

## Individual Differences in Negotiation

### Personality
- Agreeable/extraverted negotiators → **not effective** in distributive bargaining.
- Overly nice people give in too quickly.
- Intelligence predicts effectiveness, but effect is weak.

### Moods & Emotions
- **Anger in distributive** settings → can get concessions (if you're the boss).
- **Anger if you're subordinate** → backfires, worse outcomes.
- **Anxiety** → lower offers, exits early, worse results.
- **Positive mood in integrative** → leads to more creative, joint solutions.

### Culture
- **Masculine cultures** (e.g., Germany) → assertive, competitive.
- **Uncertainty-avoiding cultures** (e.g., Japan, China) → rely on rules and procedures.
- **Ambiguity-tolerant cultures** (e.g., North America, Scandinavia) → free-flowing discussion.
- **Collectivist cultures** → seek group input, slower process.
- **Individualist cultures** → self-interest first, faster but less collaborative.

### Gender
- Women tend to **under-negotiate** (only 7% negotiate first salary vs. 57% of men).
- Women penalised for being assertive due to social expectations.
- Women more hesitant, less confident before negotiations.
- Women negotiate more aggressively **for others** than for themselves.

---

## Situational Factors of Negotiation

- **Location:** Familiar location = more comfortable = better outcomes; beware of "flaming" in email negotiations.
- **Physical Setting:** Face-to-face seating increases win-lose orientation.
- **Time & Deadlines:** More time = stronger commitment; deadlines speed up concessions.
- **Audience:** More observers = negotiators become more competitive and less willing to concede.

## Ethics and Negotiation

### Ethical Violations (Lewicki, 1983) — from minor to most severe:

| Violation | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| Selective Disclosure | Highlight positives, hide negatives |
| Misrepresentation | Misstate facts or your position |
| Deception & Lying | Give factually wrong information |
| False Threats/Promises | Mislead about your intentions |
| Inflicting Harm | Sabotage the other party |

### Monitoring Your Own Ethics

| Check | Question to Ask Yourself |
|-------|--------------------------|
| Learn from mistakes | Have I repeated past ethical errors? |
| Self-evaluation | Am I proud of how I'm behaving? |
| Other's perspective | How does the other party see me? |

## Third Party Negotiations

Used when direct bargaining fails.

| Role | How They Work | Effectiveness |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Mediator** | Facilitates using persuasion, suggestions; no authority to decide | ~60% success rate, 75% satisfaction |
| **Arbitrator** | Has authority to impose a decision (voluntary or compulsory) | Always reaches settlement; risk of dissatisfaction |
| **Conciliator** | Informal communication link; finds facts, decodes messages | Hard to measure; works like mediator but more informally |

- Mediator: Best when conflict is **moderate** and emotions have cooled.
- Arbitrator: Best when quick binding decision is needed.
- Common sequence: **Mediation → Arbitration** (or reverse with sealed envelope technique).

## Gandhian Approach to Conflict Resolution

Based on **Satyagraha** — nonviolent resistance and humanistic values.

### 3 Levels of Norms

**Level 1 — Goals & Conflict:**
- Act from conviction; define conflict clearly; emphasise common goals; stay positive.

**Level 2 — Conflict Struggle & Process:**
- Act nonviolently (no harm in words, deeds, thoughts).
- Act openly, not secretly.
- Do not cooperate with evil structures.
- Do not escalate; do not humiliate.

**Level 3 — Conflict Resolution:**
- Seek negotiation and social transformation.
- Compromise on non-essentials; hold firm on essentials.
- Be generous with opponents; trust them.
- Aim for **conversion** (change of heart), not coercion.

> Core principle: *Unity of humans* — conflict is a gift and an opportunity for growth.

## Avoiding Common Mistakes in Negotiation

| Mistake | What to Do Instead |
|---------|-------------------|
| Failing to negotiate / accepting first offer | Always negotiate; people who do earn ~₹1M more over a career |
| Letting ego get in the way | Focus on the other party's goals too; use active listening |
| Unrealistic expectations | Set goals big enough to cover both sides' key interests |
| Getting overly emotional | Anger/negative words reduce chance of agreement significantly |
| Letting past failures affect present | Each negotiation is fresh; don't let history repeat itself |
