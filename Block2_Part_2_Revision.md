# MBA Revision Notes — Unit 6: Perception and Attribution

**Overview:** Perception is how individuals select, organise, and interpret information from their environment. This unit covers the perception process, factors that shape it, social perception errors, impression management, and attribution theory — all critical for understanding behaviour in organisations.

---

## 6.1 Introduction

- Each person sees the world differently; behaviour is driven by *perceived* reality, not objective reality.
- Managers and subordinates often live in different perceptual worlds, creating workplace misunderstandings.

---

## 6.2 Definition and Nature of Perception

- **Perception** = the process of receiving, selecting, organising, and interpreting environmental information to make it meaningful.
- It is a **complex cognitive process** — what we perceive may differ greatly from actual reality.
- Individual differences in processing information result in unique perceptions.

### Nature of Perception
- Perception is a *unique interpretation*, not an identical recording of reality.
- Many organisational problems (communication, conflict) are actually problems of perception.

---

## 6.3 Basic Elements in the Perceptual Process

### Bottom-Up vs Top-Down Processing

```
SENSORY INPUT (raw data)
        |
        v
  [BOTTOM-UP] Selective Attention → filters irrelevant data
        |
        v
  [TOP-DOWN]  Perceptual Organisation → finds order & meaning
        |
        v
     Interpretation (sense-making)
        |
        v
  Behavioural Response
```

- **Bottom-up processing** = handles raw sensory data; filters out noise.
- **Top-down processing** = uses knowledge/experience to fill gaps and make meaning.
- We respond to *patterns* of stimuli, not just single features.

### Selective Attention
- **External factors:** Stimulus factors (large, bright, loud, moving, unfamiliar) + Context factors.
- **Internal factors:** Learning (past experience → perceptual sets), Personality, Motivation.

### Perceptual Sets & Perceptual Worlds
- **Perceptual set** = an individual's predisposition to respond to events in a particular way (also called a *mental set*).
- Two people can observe the same event and perceive it differently — both views can be equally legitimate.
- Example: Union vs. management perceiving the same pay dispute in opposite ways.

---

## 6.4 Factors Influencing Perception

### The Perceiver
| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Personal traits | Shape what is noticed and interpreted |
| Attitude | Same situation seen differently (e.g., introvert vs. extrovert in a large class) |
| Unsatisfied needs/motives | Hungry people see food images more often |
| Interest/profession | A fashion designer notices outfits, not traffic |
| Past experience | Narrows or expands focus |
| Expectations | We tend to see what we expect to see |

### The Target
- **Figure-ground separation** = we separate an object from its background to perceive it.
- **Physical/time proximity** = nearby or temporally close events get grouped together (even if unrelated).
- **Similarity** = similar objects/people are perceived as belonging to the same group.

### The Situation
- Time, location, light, heat, work environment, social environment all shape perception.
- Example: A small mistake is more noticeable on the day the MD calls a big meeting.

---

## 6.5 Perceptual Organisation

**Definition:** The process of grouping environmental stimuli into recognisable patterns.

### Forms of Perceptual Organisation

#### 1. Figure-Ground Principle
- Perceived objects stand out from their background.
- Darker/contrasting objects in a group get noticed first.

#### 2. Perceptual Grouping (Wertheimer's Principles)
```
a) Continuity  → objects perceived as continuous patterns
b) Closure     → incomplete objects "filled in" to form a whole
c) Proximity   → nearby objects grouped together
d) Similarity  → similar objects seen as one group
```

#### 3. Perceptual Constancy
- The size, shape, colour, and location of an object remain *constant* in our mind despite changing sensory data.
- Allows experts (accountants, plumbers) to work efficiently in familiar contexts.

#### 4. Perceptual Context
- Highest form of perceptual organisation; gives meaning to stimuli.
- Same gesture (smile, raised eyebrow) means something specific *within* an organisational context.

#### 5. Perceptual Defence
- The tendency to block or refuse to recognise stimuli that feel threatening or culturally unacceptable.

## 6.6 Social Perception

- **Social/Person Perception** = the process of attributing characteristics or traits to other people.
- Influenced by: (1) characteristics of the *perceiver*, (2) characteristics of the *perceived*, (3) the *situation*.

### Characteristics of the Perceiver
- Self-aware people perceive others more accurately.
- Your own traits affect what traits you notice in others.
- People with high self-acceptance see more positives in others.

### Characteristics of the Perceived
- Status, role, and visible attributes all shape how others perceive that person.

### Problems in Social Perception (Errors)

| Error | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Halo Effect** | Judging all traits based on one trait | Beautiful candidate assumed to be competent |
| **Stereotyping** | Assigning traits based on group membership | "He's a politician, so he must be corrupt" |
| **Projection** | Seeing your own traits in others | A lazy person thinks everyone else is lazy |
| **Expectancy** | Perceiving what you expected to perceive (self-fulfilling prophecy) | Expecting lawyers to be unscrupulous — and finding it |

---

## 6.7 Impression Management

- **Definition:** The deliberate attempt by individuals to control the impressions others form of them.
- Two facets:
  - **Impression motivation** = how much a person wants to manage impressions.
  - **Impression construction** = choosing what image to project and how.

### Strategies

```
DEMOTION-PREVENTIVE (minimise blame)     PROMOTION-ENHANCING (maximise credit)
- Justification of action                - Entitlements (claiming credit)
- Forwarding an apology                  - Enhancement (deserving more credit)
- Disassociation (distance from failure) - Obstacle disclosure (overcame challenges)
                                         - Association (connect with success/people)
```

- Organisations also do impression management → called **corporate image**.

## 6.8 Attribution

- **Attribution** = the process of explaining the causes of events or behaviours.
- Developed by **Fritz Heider** and **Harold Kelley** in the 1950s–60s.

### Internal vs External Causality
```
Success of a colleague:
  Internal attribution → "She's talented and hardworking."
  External attribution → "She got lucky / has the right contacts."
```

### Three Factors in Attribution Theory
```
1. Distinctiveness  → Does the person behave this way only in this situation?
2. Consensus       → Do others behave the same way in this situation?
3. Consistency     → Does the person behave this way every time?
```

### Fundamental Attribution Error
- When judging **others**: we overestimate internal (personality) factors and ignore external (situational) factors.
- When judging **ourselves**: we credit our own success to internal factors but blame failures on external factors.

### Types of Attribution
- **Dispositional (internal):** personality traits, motivation, ability.
- **Situational (external):** social pressure, equipment, luck.

### Managerial Implication
- If poor performance → attributed to **internal** factors → coach or train the employee.
- If poor performance → attributed to **external** factors → fix the environment or resources.

## 6.9 Perception: Errors and Remedies

### Common Errors
- Not gathering enough information before judging.
- Relying on irrelevant or early information.
- Seeing only what we expect to see.
- Accepting stereotypes without questioning them.
- Letting our own traits colour how we see others.

### Remedies
- Slow down; avoid snap judgements.
- Gather more data about people before deciding.
- Build self-awareness — know your own biases.
- Check your attributions for accuracy.

### Performance Appraisal Errors (Furnham, 1997)
| Error | Description |
|---|---|
| Central tendency | Rates everyone in the middle |
| Halo / Horn effect | One good/bad trait inflates/deflates entire rating |
| Initial impression | First impression dominates the whole appraisal |
| Latest behaviour | Only recent actions are considered |
| Lenient rating | Consistently generous to avoid conflict |
| Spillover effect | Previous appraisal result bleeds into current one |
| Status effect | Higher-level employees always rated better |
| Same/Different-as-me | Biased by similarity to appraiser |

## 6.10 Perception and Its Application in Organisations

| Application Area | Key Insight |
|---|---|
| **Employment Interview** | Different interviewers perceive the same candidate differently |
| **Performance Appraisal** | Subjective impressions heavily shape ratings |
| **Effort Assessment** | Judging effort is subjective and bias-prone |
| **Loyalty Assessment** | Managers' perception of loyalty affects decisions |
| **Productivity** | Workers' perception of their job affects output more than the job itself |
| **Absenteeism/Turnover** | Negative job perception leads to absence and quitting |
| **Job Satisfaction** | Satisfaction is perceptual — positivity must be cultivated |

### Enhancing Perceptual Skills
1. Know yourself accurately.
2. Be empathetic — see things from others' perspective.
3. Keep positive attitudes to reduce distortion.
4. Strengthen self-concept.
5. Consciously avoid common biases.
6. Communicate with employees to correct wrong perceptions.
7. Avoid hasty attributions.
