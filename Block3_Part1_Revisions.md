## Unit 8: Group Dynamics and Team Building

> Groups and teams are core to how organisations achieve goals. This unit covers how groups form, what makes them productive, how teams differ from groups, and what can go wrong in teamwork.

---

### 8.1 Introduction

- Working in groups makes complex tasks easier, faster, and more creative than working alone.
- Groups bring multiple perspectives; individuals can be more productive and feel more supported in a group setting.

---

### 8.2 The Concept and Nature of Group

- A **group** = two or more people who interact, are interdependent, and share a common goal.
- Key definitions:
  - Farnham (2005): persons who communicate regularly, share goals, and build emotional bonds over time.
  - Johns (1996): two or more people interacting interdependently to achieve a common goal.
  - Hersey et al. (1998): group existence is necessary to satisfy individual members' needs.
- Groups matter in organisations because they achieve larger organisational goals, allow democratic participation, and handle complex tasks.

#### Formal vs Informal Groups

```
GROUPS
  |
  |--- FORMAL (created by org)
  |       |--- Task Forces (temporary, task-specific)
  |       |--- Committees (more permanent)
  |
  |--- INFORMAL (emerge naturally)
          |--- Interest Groups
          |--- Friendship Groups
          |--- Recreational Groups
```

- **Formal groups** are deliberately set up by the organisation to meet its goals.
- **Informal groups** arise from shared interests; can help OR hurt organisational functioning.

---

### 8.3 Stages of Group Development (Tuckman's 5-Stage Model)

```
FORMING → STORMING → NORMING → PERFORMING → ADJOURNING
```

| Stage        | What Happens                                      | Leader's Role         |
|--------------|---------------------------------------------------|-----------------------|
| Forming      | Members meet; ambiguity about purpose/roles       | Guide & clarify       |
| Storming     | Conflict, power struggles, cliques form           | Coach members         |
| Norming      | Roles clear; consensus builds; focus on task      | Facilitate            |
| Performing   | Mature group; clear vision; resolves disagreements | Delegate & oversee    |
| Adjourning   | Task done; group disbands; members feel achieved  | Recognise contributions|

### 8.4 Determinants of Group Productivity

Key determinants (McAfee & Champagne, 1987): **Group Size, Composition, Norms, Cohesiveness, Leadership**

#### Group Size
- Larger group = more resources BUT harder to coordinate.
- Smaller group = easier to manage BUT fewer ideas.
- **Span of control** = how many subordinates a leader can effectively supervise.
- Goal: find the **optimal size** that balances resources and coordination.

#### Group Composition
- Refers to similarity/dissimilarity of members (age, gender, skills, background).
- **Homogeneous group** → better for simple tasks.
- **Heterogeneous group** → better for complex tasks (diverse views).
- Desirable personal traits: stable, responsible, dependable, self-reliant.

**FIRO-B Theory** (Fundamental Interpersonal Relations Orientation):
- Focuses on how people adjust to others; groups with **compatible needs** perform better.

```
3 Compatible Needs:
  Inclusion  →  need to interact with others
  Control    →  need to influence others' behaviour
  Affection  →  need for closeness with others
```

#### Group Norms
- **Norms** = unwritten/written behaviour standards that all members are expected to follow.
- Guirdham's 4 Key Norms:
  1. **Fairness** — equal contributions from all members.
  2. **Reciprocity** — members return help given to them.
  3. **Reasonableness** — rational, non-exploitative behaviour.
  4. **Role Expectation** — clarity on what role each member plays.
- Norms regulate behaviour, express group values, and guide against deviance.
- Violation of norms → punishment; compliance → reward.

#### Group Cohesiveness
- **Cohesion** = the degree to which members are attracted to the group and to each other (Nahavandi et al., 2015).
- Also called "the cement that holds the group together."
- Festinger et al. (1950): cohesion = total forces causing members to stay in the group.

**High Cohesiveness → Positive Outcomes:**
```
High Cohesion
    ↓
Better coordination + interaction
    ↓
Higher satisfaction, morale, motivation
    ↓
Lower absenteeism & turnover + higher goal achievement
```

Factors influencing cohesion: similarity, trust, openness, past success/failure, group incentives.

#### Group Leadership
- Leader directs group toward goals and significantly influences member behaviour.
- Leadership style depends on the **nature of the group and task**.
- Informal group leaders are as important as formal ones in an organisation.

### 8.5 Teams: Nature and Types

- A **team** = a more mature, highly interdependent group with complementary skills, a common purpose, and shared accountability (Moorhead & Griffin, 2010).

#### Groups vs Teams — Quick Comparison

```
GROUPS                          TEAMS
--------------------------------------------
Two or more members             Smaller, more focused
Less interdependent             Highly interdependent
Less sense of common purpose    Strong shared purpose
Lower accountability            Higher accountability
Less mature                     More mature
```

#### Types of Teams

| Team Type              | Key Feature                                                   |
|------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Work Teams             | Permanent; work together daily (e.g. marketing team)         |
| Cross-Functional Teams | Members from different departments, same hierarchy level      |
| Top Management Teams   | Small; senior leaders; high individuality + interdependence   |
| Process-Improvement Teams | Focused on improving work processes                        |
| Project Teams          | Like cross-functional but members engaged at different stages |
| Self-Managed Teams     | High autonomy; no fixed leader; risk of lacking guidance      |
| Virtual Teams          | Geographically dispersed; use technology to collaborate       |
| Quality Circles        | Small group; rotating leadership; focus on quality & safety   |

#### Team Activities (what teams do)
Advising → Innovating → Promoting → Developing → Organising → Producing → Inspecting → Maintaining → Linking

### 8.6 Effective Team Building

> Team building = interventions designed to improve work group effectiveness (Quick et al., 2015).

Key aspects to consider when building an effective team:

- **Team Size** — Ideal size: 4–12 members; too large = coordination issues + social loafing; too small = fewer ideas.
- **Team Composition** — Choose homogeneous or heterogeneous based on task type.
- **Clear SMART Goals** — Goals must be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Reasonable, Time-bound.
- **Team Cohesiveness** — Select members who can work together; high cohesion → better productivity, less conflict.
- **Diversity Management** — Diverse teams bring rich ideas; must be managed to avoid conflict.
- **Trust** — Defined as each member's faith in others' intentions and behaviours (Nahavandi et al., 2015); essential for coordination and collaboration.
- **Leadership** — Leader must know team goals, member roles, and motivate/guide/support the team; rotation-based leadership is also an option.
- **Training** — Provide technical and behavioural skills training (communication, teamwork, interpersonal skills).
- **Conflict Management** — Disagreements are normal but must be resolved quickly to protect team performance.

### 8.7 Problems Affecting Teamwork

| Problem                             | Impact                                                        |
|-------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Changes in team composition         | Breaks rapport and cohesion                                   |
| Poor interpersonal relationships    | Reduces cooperation and goal achievement                      |
| Communication gaps                  | Misunderstandings damage relationships                        |
| Social loafing                      | Some members don't contribute; lowers morale & performance    |
| Ineffective leadership              | Poor coordination, collaboration, and direction               |
| Role ambiguity                      | Members unsure of their responsibilities                      |
| Lack of trust                       | Limits information sharing and collaboration                  |
| Domination by one/few members       | Silences others; reduces quality of ideas                     |
| Low motivation and morale           | Members don't work toward team goals                          |
| Disagreements over goals/activities | Disrupts functioning if not resolved quickly                  |

#### Strategies to Address These Problems

1. Maintain optimal team size for good coordination.
2. Select members with **technical**, **problem-solving**, and **interpersonal skills**.
3. Encourage **open communication** at all levels.
4. Keep **goals and roles clear** at all times.
5. Promote **shared leadership** wherever possible.
6. Build respectful behaviour: listening, positive attitude, no blame games.
7. Encourage **consensus-based decisions**.
8. Create a **supportive organisational environment**.
9. Train members in **stress and time management**.
10. Provide **regular upskilling** in technical and behavioural areas.
